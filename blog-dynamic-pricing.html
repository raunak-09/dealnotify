The Price You See Isn't Always
the Price You Pay

How big retailers use pricing algorithms to charge you more — and how to fight back.

🔔 DealNotify Team · 📅 April 2026 · ⏱ 6 min read

You find a product you've been eyeing for weeks. The price looks reasonable. You add it to your cart, tell yourself you'll buy it tomorrow, and go to bed. By morning? The price is $15 higher. This isn't a coincidence. It's an algorithm — and it's working exactly as intended.

Retailers like Amazon, Walmart, Best Buy, and Target have spent billions building pricing systems that adjust costs in real time based on what you're doing, where you are, how much you want something, and how likely you are to pay more. The playing field isn't level. But it doesn't have to stay that way.

Amazon's Pricing Algorithm Never Sleeps

Amazon adjusts product prices 2.5 million times per day — roughly one price change every 10 minutes across its catalogue. For context, that's 50 times more frequently than Walmart. And it's not random.

The algorithm is constantly reading signals from across the internet and making micro-decisions about every single product:

  • High demand, low stock? Price goes up — automatically, sometimes within minutes.
  • A competitor dropped their price? Amazon responds almost instantly.
  • You've been browsing the same product? Your return visits signal purchase intent — and the price may quietly rise to meet it.
  • It's 2am on a Tuesday? Different time of day, different demand pattern, potentially different price.
  • Low demand, heavy inventory? Prices dip to move stock — often with zero fanfare or announcement.

"Checking a price once and hoping it stays there is like checking the weather this morning and assuming it'll be the same all week."

— DealNotify Team

Amazon gets most of the headlines, but dynamic pricing has spread to every major retailer. Around 61% of retailers now use some form of dynamic pricing, and AI-powered versions are rapidly becoming the standard.

"Sale" Doesn't Always Mean What You Think

⚠️

Research has found that 22% of items displaying a reduced "list price" are actually associated with a net price increase — the retailer inflated the original price before marking it "on sale." That crossed-out price you feel good about beating? It may never have been real.

This practice is especially common during Black Friday, Prime Day, and other high-traffic shopping events — exactly when consumers are most emotionally primed to spend and least likely to question whether a deal is genuinely a deal.

The FTC took formal notice in January 2025, releasing findings from its Surveillance Pricing Study that confirmed major retailers are using a wide range of personal data — including location, browsing history, purchase behaviour, and device type — to set individualised prices for individual customers.

🔍

A Consumer Reports investigation found that three-quarters of products on Instacart showed different prices to different customers, with variations ranging from a few cents to $2.56 per item. A Samsung TV was priced at $877.95 for one shopper and $1,047.90 for another in a different city. Same TV. Same website. $170 difference.

The Psychology Behind It All

Retailers don't just use data science — they use behavioural psychology, and they're very good at it.

Research consistently shows that loss aversion makes a price increase feel roughly twice as painful as a discount of the same size feels good. A 10% price hike registers as a genuine loss. A 10% discount feels nice — but not as urgent. This is why "limited time" warnings and "only 3 left in stock" badges are plastered across every product page. Urgency is manufactured.

A 2019 investigation found that Target app users were shown higher prices when physically inside a Target store compared to users outside in the car park. The logic is ruthlessly simple: if you're already inside, you're more committed. You're more likely to pay. The algorithm knows this.

"If you're already inside the store, you're more committed. The algorithm knows this — and the price adjusts accordingly."

— Dynamic Pricing Research, 2019

Dynamic pricing is a strategy where retailers automatically adjust prices in real time based on demand, stock levels, competitor pricing, time of day, and customer behaviour. Amazon pioneered it in e-commerce and today around 61% of major retailers use some form of it.
